# Intervention Rules

This document defines when and how Harvey may intervene in the Twin's behavior, output flow, or governance evolution.

Intervention exists to preserve safety, clarity, continuity, and creative health.
It is not a failure condition by default.

The Twin is exploratory, but it is not fully self-governing.
Harvey remains the supervising authority.

---

## Watchouts

### Intervention as Constant Micromanagement

Too much intervention can flatten emergence and make the Twin overly reactive to short-term human preference.

Intervene when needed, not reflexively.

### Waiting Too Long to Intervene

The opposite failure is allowing drift, repetition, confusion, or risky rule adoption to continue unchecked.

Supervision should remain active enough to prevent avoidable damage.

### Treating Intervention as Deletion

Intervention often means redirect, pause, annotate, hold, or tighten a rule.
It does not always mean erase or reject.

### Mixing Creative Intervention with Governance Intervention

Stopping a weak artifact and rejecting a policy proposal are not the same kind of event.
The system should preserve that distinction.

---

## 1. Purpose of Intervention Rules

Intervention Rules define the boundaries of supervised control over the Twin.

They help answer:

- when should Harvey step in?
- what kinds of intervention are valid?
- what should happen after intervention?
- when must a governance or runtime change be blocked until review?
- what should be recorded after meaningful intervention?

Without intervention rules, the Twin may drift or behave inconsistently.
With overly aggressive intervention, the Twin may lose exploratory value.

The goal is guided evolution.

---

## 2. What Counts as Intervention

An intervention is any meaningful supervisory action that changes, pauses, redirects, constrains, or blocks the Twin's current or proposed behavior.

Examples:

- pausing a session direction
- stopping a repetitive generation loop
- forcing review before publication
- rejecting a proposed governance change
- redirecting the medium or task category
- adding annotation that changes future interpretation
- tightening a threshold or rule boundary
- preventing a proposal from becoming active

Not every comment is an intervention.
A true intervention changes behavior, state flow, or governance outcome.

---

## 3. Intervention Domains

In V1, interventions usually fall into four domains.

### A. Creative flow intervention
Used when the current session or artifact path is weak, repetitive, unstable, or directionally off.

### B. Review and publication intervention
Used when an artifact should not move forward to external surfaces without added review, revision, or annotation.

### C. Governance intervention
Used when a proposed rule, policy, or identity change should be blocked, delayed, or revised.

### D. Structural intervention
Used when larger runtime behavior needs tighter control because the system is creating confusion, drift, or poor continuity.

---

## 4. When Harvey Should Intervene

Recommended triggers for intervention include:

### Repetition without discovery
The Twin keeps producing slight variations without meaningful emergence.

### Identity drift
Outputs begin contradicting the approved identity or philosophy in a way that looks unstable rather than exploratory.

### Evaluation distortion
Signals are clearly overvaluing novelty, productivity, or recurrence in a way that degrades quality.

### Approval confusion
Artifacts or proposals are reaching publication or active status without the right review path.

### Governance overreach
A proposed system change would reduce supervision, blur approval requirements, or create silent autonomy.

### Session exhaustion or collapse
The session appears stuck, noisy, or unable to produce useful movement.

### Surface mismatch
An artifact may be valuable internally but inappropriate for staging or public release.

---

## 5. Valid Intervention Actions

Recommended V1 intervention actions:

### Pause
Stop the current direction without necessarily rejecting the underlying idea.

### Redirect
Change medium, task type, project focus, or session mode.

### Require revision
Send the artifact or proposal back for a more complete or safer form.

### Hold for review
Prevent movement into active or published status until Harvey decides.

### Annotate
Add interpretive or cautionary context that must stay attached.

### Archive
Move the item out of active flow while preserving return potential.

### Reject adoption
For governance changes or activation proposals that should not become active.

### Tighten rule boundaries
Change thresholds, approval requirements, or review gates when supervision needs strengthening.

---

## 6. Intervention Severity

A lightweight severity model helps avoid overreaction.

### Level 1 — soft intervention
Examples:
- annotation
- gentle redirect
- hold for later review

Use when the issue is real but not damaging.

### Level 2 — active intervention
Examples:
- require revision
- pause the current path
- archive the artifact or thread from active flow

Use when continuation would likely waste energy or create confusion.

### Level 3 — governance intervention
Examples:
- reject a policy change
- block activation of a new rule
- tighten approval requirements

Use when system-level drift or unsafe autonomy is at stake.

V1 does not need a formal severity enum yet, but this mental model is useful.

---

## 7. Intervention and Recordkeeping

Meaningful interventions should usually create preserved context.

Recommended V1 behavior:

- artifact-level intervention should be visible in approval history, annotation, or archive context
- governance intervention should usually produce a change record if it materially alters system behavior
- rejected governance proposals may still be preserved historically
- interventions that change future rules should be versioned and recorded

This keeps intervention from becoming invisible oral tradition.

---

## 8. Intervention vs Self Critique vs Evaluation vs Approval

These layers must remain distinct.

### Self critique
The Twin's internal qualitative judgment.

### Evaluation
The runtime's structured interpretation.

### Approval
Harvey's review status for an artifact or proposal.

### Intervention
A supervisory action that changes what happens next.

An intervention may influence approval.
An approval decision may function as intervention.
But conceptually they are not identical.

Example:

- critique says the artifact is repetitive
- evaluation shows low emergence and low fertility
- Harvey intervenes by redirecting the medium
- the artifact remains preserved but is not approved for publication

That sequence is healthy and distinct.

---

## 9. Rules for Governance Proposals

The following proposed changes should be blocked from automatic activation and require explicit Harvey review:

- constitution changes
- approval logic changes
- intervention rule changes
- evaluation interpretation changes
- stop-limit changes
- publication gate changes
- identity changes that affect runtime interpretation
- memory behavior changes that materially alter retrieval or weighting

No governance proposal should self-activate simply because it was generated inside the system.

---

## 10. Rules for Creative Sessions

During active creative work, Harvey should usually intervene only when one of the following is true:

- the session is clearly stuck
- the output is collapsing into repetition
- the system is drifting away from approved boundaries
- the current path is no longer producing insight
- the artifact is heading toward an inappropriate surface or release path

Otherwise, allow exploration enough room to reveal something real.

---

## 11. V1 Canonical Rules

For V1, the following rules should hold:

- Harvey may intervene in creative, review, governance, and structural domains
- interventions should prefer redirect, pause, hold, annotate, or archive before destructive deletion
- governance proposals must not self-activate
- interventions that materially change future system behavior should be recorded and versioned
- intervention should preserve exploration where possible while preventing silent drift or unsafe adoption

---

## 12. Canonical Summary

Intervention Rules define how supervision enters the system when exploration alone is not enough.

They exist to make the Twin:

- governable
- redirectable
- safer to evolve
- less prone to silent drift
- still capable of meaningful emergence
